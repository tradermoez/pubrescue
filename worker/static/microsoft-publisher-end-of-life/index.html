Microsoft Publisher End of Life: What October 2026 Means for Your .pub Files

Updated June 2026

After 35 years, Microsoft Publisher is being retired. Microsoft's official guidance says it plainly: Publisher reaches end of life on October 1, 2026, and after that date Microsoft 365 subscribers will no longer be able to open or edit Publisher files. Support and security updates end at the same time.

Who this actually affects

Publisher was the quiet workhorse of small organizations. The people hit hardest by this retirement are the ones least likely to have an IT department:

  • Churches with years of weekly bulletins, newsletters, and event flyers
  • Schools and PTAs with newsletters, certificates, and programs
  • Clubs and nonprofits with member communications and posters
  • Small businesses with menus, price lists, brochures, and signage

If any of that sounds like you, the files themselves are the issue: .pub is a proprietary format that almost nothing else on Earth can open. The day Publisher stops working, every one of those documents becomes a locked box.

The timeline

What Microsoft tells you to do

Microsoft's own recommendation is to convert your Publisher files to other formats — primarily PDF — before the deadline, and to use Word, PowerPoint, or Designer for new documents. That advice is sound, but it comes with a catch: the built-in "Save as PDF" works one file at a time, on one computer that still has Publisher installed, operated by someone with the patience to do it hundreds of times.

Your practical options

1. Convert each file manually in Publisher (free, slow)

If you still have a working Publisher installation and a manageable number of files: File → Export → Create PDF/XPS, once per document. For 10 files this is fine. For 300 bulletins, it's a lost weekend.

Frequently asked questions

Can I just rename .pub to .docx?

No. The file extension isn't the format — a .pub file is a proprietary binary container that Word cannot read, renamed or not.

Does Word or PowerPoint open .pub files?

No Microsoft Office application other than Publisher itself has ever opened .pub files. Microsoft's migration guidance is about creating new documents in those apps, not opening old ones.

How do I keep my designs editable after Publisher is gone?

Convert to ODG (OpenDocument Graphics). It opens in LibreOffice Draw — free, maintained, and available on Windows, Mac, and Linux. PubRescue includes ODG in every conversion batch.

What about complex multi-page newsletters?
